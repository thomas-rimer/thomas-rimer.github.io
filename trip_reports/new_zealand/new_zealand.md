We left the night of the 26th on a direct flight from SFO to Christchurch (on the South Island). The flight was about 17 hours, but went surprisingly well. I had a few movies downloaded and watched a lot of redownload movie. We didn’t realize it until we landed, but New Zealand is 21 hours ahead of the Bay Area, which means in practice it’s only 3 hours behind. So when we landed we actually felt alright—and we never had jet lag!

## Day 1 - Arthurs Pass
We landed around 10am on the 28th and immediately hoped in our rental car to get on the move. We drove about 2 hours up to Arthurs pass, where we went on three short hikes. Arthur’s pass is a few thousand feet up, so while the pass isn’t super high altitude, the mountains which it cuts through very much are. It’s quintessential galcier-meets-jungle. Our first hike was a super cool cluster of rocks on the side of the road on the mountain road up to Arthur’s pass. The next hike was a short (~1 mile) hike up to a waterfall, where we managed to get drenched but still had a good time. The last hike was much longer, and followed a river bed up to the toe of a huge glacier. Much of the hike was spent wading through the glacial water, which was pretty cold, but worth it for the views. That evening we drove down from Arthurs pass and got a late dinner in a super small coastal town. Then we continued to drive down the west coast on what was one of the best roads I’ve ever been on. The west coast of New Zealand is extremely remote (very often driving for hours without encountering another town) so we felt like we were just driving through the wilderness. That night we got into our hotel at Fox Glacier just before midnight.

## Day 2 - West Coast Road Trip
The next day we had breakfast at a cafe and got an early start. We drove about 2 hours further down the west coast of New Zealand to a beach we had read about that morning. We pulled off on the side of the road in a dirt parking lot and then just starting walking through the jungle. It ended up being about an hour walk through some of the thickest jungle I’ve ever seen to a totally untouched, white sand beach with super cool rock features in the water. We spent over an hour just walking up and down the beach, checking out the caves and watching the dolphins. However, we had many more hours of driving ahead of us so we eventually had to head back through the jungle to our car. We then drove another 3 hours, again on some of the prettiest roads I’d ever been on, to the town of Wanaka. Wanaka is like the outdoor capital of New Zealand, and has a really famous peak just outside of downtown called Roys peak. It’s a 3 mile hike one way, but climbs over 4,000 vertical feet (double the height of Palisades ski resort tram). We rolled in around 5pm and started hiking at top speed. Usually the hike takes about 4 hours one way but we run the top section, finishing in under 1.5 hours. The views from he top were unreal—we had perfectly blue skies and could see for dozens of miles in all directions. We hiked down as the sun set, which was even cooler. We got back to our car around 8, and drove into town for dinner. We got some burgers before heading out on another 3 hour drive from Wanaka to the small town of Te Anau. We ended up getting in around 2am, having hiked for 6 hours and driven for nearly 9 hours.

## Day 3 - Kepler Track
Despite the long day prior, this day and Te Anau would prove to be the longest of the trip. We woke up on the earlier side, got some breakfast at a grocery store in Te Anau, and made the short drive to the Kepler Track trailhead. The Kepler Track is one of the most famous hikes in New Zealand, usually completed over the course of a few days (it’s ~36 miles in length). We decided to make it into an out and back. We started hiking at 10 am. The hike started with about 4 miles of flat along the short of a lake, followed by a gigantic 2,000 foot climb up past the tree line. Then the hike opened up revealing some of the prettiest high-alpine mountains I’d ever seen. We continued to hike for many more miles, passing several huts and a few other people (though it was surprisingly uncrowded). Ww ended up having our packed lunch about 15 miles into the hike. At that point, we turned around and did it in reverse. The hike ended up clocking in around 32 miles, and took just over 10 hours. We were exhausted when we got back to the car but it was one of the greatest hikes I’ve ever been on. But we weren’t even close to being done for the day. I quickly went swimming in the lake to clean off, then we drove into town to get some pizza. Then we made the 3 hour drive back to Wanaka (where we had been the day before). We got into Wanaka around 2 am, having hiked over 30 miles and driven nearly 4 hours that day. I cannot recall a time where I’ve been that tired in a long time. In Wanaka we stayed in a super nice “tiny home” Airbnb which was super fun.

## Day 4 - Rest Day
After two crazy long days, we took a well needed rest day (it was also new years eve). We went grocery shopping (for our upcoming hut trip) and then went to buy some backpacking supplies. We had lunch in Wanaka, before making the 2 hour drive to the trail head where we would begin our hike the following day. The trail head was only accessible from a ~10 mile dirt road with half a dozen river crossings. Concerned about how our rental corolla would do, we decided to do some reconnaissance on the road, and practice crossing rivers in the car. We ended up walking about 3 miles scouting out the road, in one of the greatest valleys I’d ever been. There were thousands of sheep everywhere, and about 100 waterfalls in all directions. That evening we drove back to our house, cleaned up, and went downtown for new years dinner. We went to an awesome Indian restaurant right on the water, and got a view on their deck overlooking the lake. That night, a new years eve concert sprung up outside our restaurant (with a few thousand people there) which was fun. We ended up watching the New Years fireworks before heading home.

## Day 5 - Up to the French Ridge Hut
Today we set out on our hut trip. In the morning we packed our backpacks, assembled our gear, and cooked our food for the next 24 hours. We got breakfast at the grocery store, and then set out back to the trail head which we had scoped out the day prior. We crossed all the streams without any (significant) issues and made it to the trail head around 1. We set out on our 12 mile hike up to the French Ridge hut, a backcountry hut (without heating, electricity, running water, etc…). The first ~9 miles were flat, following a giant glacier run-off river through some of the prettiest valleys I’ve ever been. It started raining towards the end of this flat section but was still super nice. Then began the absolutely crazy climb up through the jungle to the hut. Despite being barely more than a mile long, the trail climbed over 3,000 vertical feet through some of the densest jungle terrain I’d ever been. For literally thousands of feet, our feet hardly touched the ground—instead we climbed the roots of trees and bushes like ladders. It took us nearly 2 hours to go this one mile, all the while the rain slowly turned the trail into a waterfall. Needless to say it was quite the climb. Finally we punched out through the jungle and climbed the final thousand feet along a stunning ridge, until finally we reached the hut. There were 8 other people there. Everyone slept in a 20 person bunk room. In addition to the bunk room is also gear vestibule room, where you hang all your wet gear, and a kitchen/dining room. By the time we got to the hut and got settled, it was pretty much time to eat dinner (precooked pasta from the morning). Without electricity or internet, we all went to sleep with the sun (around 10pm, as the sun sets crazy late in New Zealand in the summer).

## Day 6 - Down from the French Ridge Hut
Having spent the night in the hut, we woke up to a breakfast of toast and peanut butter, before putting on our wet clothes to begin the descent back down. It was still damp from the previous day, but at least it wasn’t raining. The descent was just as challenging as the ascent, but we were able to make good time. Once we reached the bottom, we began the long walk back to the car. Though we were going in reverse along the same trail we had just hiked the day prior, it was nonetheless stunning. We took a long break on the shore of the glacial river, and made one final push to the car. We made it back in the early afternoon, changed into dry and clean clothes, and began our drive back across the half dozen rivers, to the road to Mt. Cook. The drive was a few hours, and once again was filled with stunning views. Unfortunately, thick fog in Mt. Cook prevented us from seeing much when we got there. That night we were staying in a hostel, so we cleaned up, grabbed dinner at the only restaurant in town, and had the first free time of the trio (all of one hour) before going to bed.

## Day 7 - Mt Cook to Lake Tekapo
We had planned on taking a helicopter tour of Mt Cook this morning, but when we woke up the fog was hanging low and not moving. We were still hopeful, so we drove to the airport and waited for our pilots input. Tragically, they ended up canceling on us, so we improvised and drove back to the town and began a hike up to the Mueller Hut, several thousand feet above the valley floor. We hoped we’d punch through the fog, but instead we just got socked in even more. By the time we got to the top, we were trudging through wet snow in shorts and a t-shirt, with rain coming down. We summited (or at least came within 100 feet, it was a little too foggy to tell for sure) and then descended down, bummed at the lack of visibility. However, as we hiked down the fog began to lift. Half way down the clouds gave way to stunning views of the valley and the bottom of the glaciers which hang off Mt. Cook. It was quite the scene. We were super happy to have finally seen the mountain, and finished out the hike in sun. Once we got back to the car, we began our drive back to Christchurch. Two hours into the drive, we stopped through Lake Tekapo, a bright blue lake famous for its purple Lupine on its shores. We got crepes for lunch and walked around the town. That night we drove into Christchurch, finishing off the trip with some Tacos in downtown Christchurch.

The following day we got up, went to the airport, and hoped on the plane back to SFO. Once again, the return flight was relatively uneventful, though the mid-day flight made it a little harder to fall asleep.
